# Samet Ozkale Reference Notes

Source:

- Site: [samet.works](https://www.samet.works/)
- About page: [samet.works/about](https://www.samet.works/about)
- Reference type: live pages + provided screenshots

## Overall Impression

This site is minimal, but it is the most "productized" of the three references.

Emil Kowalski feels editorial.
Shu Ding feels archival.
Samet feels like a polished founder-designer portfolio.

The design is still restrained, but it is more willing to:

- show product screenshots
- use soft cards
- introduce a stronger accent color
- present services and trust signals
- guide the visitor through a clearer professional narrative

It does not feel loud or overbuilt. It feels calm, competent, and commercially ready.

## Core Visual Characteristics

### 1. Narrow centered content with strong vertical rhythm

The site keeps most content inside a narrow central column, similar in spirit to Emil's site, but with more component variety inside that column.

Effect:

- preserves readability
- keeps the portfolio feeling intentional
- makes screenshots and content blocks feel curated instead of scattered
- prevents the page from turning into a wide product landing page

### 2. Softly productized minimalism

Unlike Shu, this site is not purely typographic. It uses:

- white cards
- subtle borders
- rounded corners
- faint shadows
- polished screenshots

Effect:

- the site feels modern and professional
- product work is easier to scan visually
- minimalism stays friendly instead of austere

### 3. A restrained accent color creates identity

The screenshots show a bright blue accent used sparingly in buttons and small UI moments.

Effect:

- gives the site a recognizable personality
- creates clearer calls to action
- adds warmth and momentum without breaking the clean feel

This is important because it shows that a minimal portfolio does not have to be fully monochrome to stay tasteful.

### 4. Section blocks are distinct, but still light

The homepage uses clear sections:

- intro
- selections
- trust logos
- experience
- services
- human UI
- writings
- footer

But the separation is still handled with spacing and light surfaces rather than heavy layout chrome.

Effect:

- the page is easy to scan
- the user understands the story quickly
- the site feels structured without becoming corporate

### 5. The site feels handcrafted, not templated

Even though the homepage includes common portfolio content types, it avoids feeling generic because:

- spacing is very controlled
- the screenshot framing is consistent
- typography stays modest
- the page does not over-explain each section
- decorative effects stay minimal

## Homepage Observations

The homepage is more complete and business-ready than the Emil or Shu references.

It introduces:

- who Samet is
- what kind of work he does
- selected product work
- company trust markers
- previous experience
- services
- side explorations
- writing
- footer navigation

### Tone of the homepage

The tone is:

- confident
- product-minded
- founder-friendly
- clear
- warm without oversharing

It avoids:

- vague creative-language hero copy
- exaggerated thought-leader voice
- cluttered startup-marketing sections
- overly emotional storytelling

### Intro block

The hero is still compact.

Observed pattern:

- name
- short role description
- concise present-tense context
- one or two actions

Effect:

- gets to the point quickly
- feels serious and mature
- avoids wasting the first viewport on self-branding theater

### Selections section

This section is one of the strongest references on the homepage.

The provided screenshot suggests:

- product images shown as carefully framed canvases
- enough whitespace around them
- no noisy case-study cards
- the work itself acts as the visual interest

Effect:

- product quality becomes visible immediately
- the page feels premium without using loud portfolio UI
- a small number of good visuals carries more weight than a gallery of thumbnails

### Trusted-by logos

This section is minimal and well handled.

Effect:

- adds credibility quickly
- prevents the need for long social-proof copy
- gives the page commercial trust without becoming "agency-like"

Important takeaway:

If trust signals are used, they should stay quiet and integrated like this.

### Experience section

The experience area combines short written summaries with large framed visuals.

Observed pattern:

- role / product name
- concise description
- date range
- one image or interface preview

Effect:

- strong balance between explanation and proof
- the portfolio reads like selected product evidence
- repeated structure creates calm rhythm

### Services section

This part is useful because it shows how to include service categories without ruining minimalism.

The screenshot suggests a grid of soft rectangular pills or cards such as:

- product design
- websites
- brand design
- user research
- AI strategy

Effect:

- turns broad capability into something quickly legible
- adds utility for clients or collaborators
- stays visually gentle because the treatment is light

### Human UI / side work section

This section adds personality and experimentation.

It matters because it keeps the site from feeling purely commercial.

Effect:

- shows taste and curiosity
- makes the portfolio feel alive
- broadens the identity beyond client/project history

### Writings section

The writing area is very light and list-based.

That is a good pattern:

- no heavy blog cards
- no giant article previews
- titles do most of the work

Effect:

- preserves homepage focus
- suggests depth without overwhelming the page

## About Page Observations

The about page is especially useful because it shows how Samet handles longer personal narrative without losing restraint.

### 1. The page is still narrow and quiet

The about page keeps a disciplined reading width and generous whitespace.

Effect:

- the text feels readable
- the page stays calm even though it contains more biography
- the site remains visually consistent with the homepage

### 2. The biography is direct and professional

The writing focuses on:

- experience
- leadership
- philosophy
- current direction
- what makes him different

Effect:

- the personal story feels useful, not indulgent
- the page builds confidence through specificity
- the site feels human without becoming diary-like

### 3. Timeline/history is a strong structural device

The history section on the about page is one of the most useful patterns in this reference set.

Observed pattern:

- date on the left
- event / company / explanation on the right
- many entries stacked with consistent rhythm

Effect:

- turns biography into a scan-friendly format
- gives the page a sense of progression
- avoids long uninterrupted autobiographical paragraphs

This is a very practical pattern for our own portfolio if we want to present work history cleanly.

### 4. CTA placement is modest but clear

The `Work with me` button appears without dominating the page.

Effect:

- makes the page actionable
- avoids aggressive sales energy
- keeps the site feeling personal and professional

## What Makes It Feel Non-Cliche

This reference avoids cliché portfolio behavior even though it includes more familiar portfolio sections.

Why it works:

- the hero is still compact
- the screenshots are curated and consistent
- services are shown lightly, not as big sales panels
- credibility is integrated instead of shouted
- the page does not rely on buzzword-heavy copy
- the visual language stays soft and controlled

It feels more like:

- a high-taste founder portfolio
- a product designer's working archive
- a refined professional homepage

Not like:

- an agency landing page
- a template marketplace portfolio
- a self-promotional brand page

## What To Borrow For Our Portfolio

These are the strongest ideas to adapt:

1. Keep the hero compact, even when the site has many sections.
2. Use a narrow content column with generous whitespace.
3. Let screenshots do the visual heavy lifting.
4. Use one accent color sparingly for clarity and identity.
5. Keep trust logos quiet and integrated.
6. Present experience as concise proof blocks, not long case-study cards.
7. Use a timeline for biography or history if needed.
8. Let writing stay list-based on the homepage.
9. Use light cards or pills only when they improve scannability.
10. Make the site feel warm and professional, not austere or over-marketed.

## What Not To Copy Literally

We should borrow the approach, not duplicate the site.

Avoid copying directly:

- the exact blue accent usage
- the exact section order
- the exact services grid
- the exact footer structure
- the exact founder/product tone if Mohamed wants a slightly different voice
