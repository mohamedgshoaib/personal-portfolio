# Emil Kowalski Reference Notes

Source:

- Site: [emilkowal.ski](https://emilkowal.ski/)
- Article: [Good vs Great Animations](https://emilkowal.ski/ui/good-vs-great-animations)
- Reference type: live page + full-screen screenshot

## Overall Impression

This design feels extremely calm, deliberate, and confident. It is minimal without feeling empty because the spacing, alignment, and typography are doing most of the work.

It does not try to impress through visual effects. It feels strong because:

- the layout is narrow and disciplined
- the copy is short and high-signal
- the typography is understated but precise
- the spacing creates rhythm and breathing room
- the page never looks like stacked "portfolio sections"

## Core Visual Characteristics

### 1. Narrow reading column

The content sits in a relatively narrow central column rather than stretching across the page.

Effect:

- improves readability
- makes the page feel editorial
- prevents the portfolio from feeling like a dashboard
- gives even simple text blocks a more intentional presence

### 2. Large negative space

Most of the page is empty space. This is one of the main reasons it feels premium.

Effect:

- gives each section room to stand on its own
- slows the page down in a good way
- removes the urge to over-explain
- makes the author seem more confident

### 3. Very restrained typography

Typography is modest in scale. There is no oversized hero headline dominating the page.

Observed behavior:

- small body-sized text drives most of the page
- headings are present but not loud
- link titles are slightly emphasized, not dramatically styled
- supporting text is muted but still readable

Effect:

- the page feels more like thoughtful notes than self-promotion
- avoids the cliché "I am a creative developer" landing-page tone
- creates intimacy and trust

### 4. Plain white background

The page uses a quiet, almost fully neutral background with dark text and very little decoration.

Effect:

- puts total focus on information hierarchy
- makes spacing and typography the design language
- avoids trendy gradients/noise/patterns

### 5. Weak section chrome, strong content rhythm

Sections are separated mostly by vertical spacing, not by cards, rules, panels, or backgrounds.

Observed sections:

- name / role
- short introduction
- projects
- writing
- newsletter
- more / external links

Effect:

- the page reads continuously
- avoids the "boxed sections stacked on a page" feel
- makes the portfolio feel personal rather than corporate

## Layout Notes

### Header treatment

The top area is extremely small and compact:

- just name
- role directly beneath or beside it
- no oversized hero
- no decorative portrait dominating the fold

Effect:

- instantly communicates identity
- avoids wasting the first viewport on branding theater
- gives more trust than a dramatic personal slogan

### Section rhythm

Every section has a lot of vertical separation from the previous one.

This creates:

- clean pauses between thoughts
- a strong scanning rhythm
- a sense that every block deserves attention

### List structure

Projects and writing are shown as simple vertical lists:

- title first
- one-line description below
- repeated with consistent spacing

This is important. The site does not use:

- project cards
- thumbnails
- noisy tags
- exaggerated CTA buttons
- icons everywhere

Effect:

- feels like a curated index
- keeps attention on substance
- makes the page lightweight and timeless

## Typography Notes

### Tone

The typography feels:

- neutral
- precise
- quiet
- mature

Not:

- playful
- heavily branded
- startup-marketing heavy
- overly expressive

### Hierarchy

Hierarchy comes from:

- spacing
- subtle weight changes
- muted secondary text
- sequence and grouping

Not from:

- huge font jumps
- bright colors
- badges
- decorative headings

### Copy density

The copy is concise.

The page does not explain too much. It trusts:

- short intros
- short descriptions
- clear labels

This is a major part of why it feels elegant.

## Interaction Notes

From the screenshot and site structure, the design suggests that interactions should be understated:

- links likely rely on subtle color/underline shifts
- no obvious heavy animation is needed
- minimal portfolios like this benefit from quick, nearly invisible interaction feedback

Important takeaway:

If motion exists, it should support clarity, not become a visible personality trait of the page.

## Content Strategy Notes

This site works because the information is selective.

It highlights:

- current work
- a short previous-work note
- a few projects
- a writing index
- newsletter
- external links

It does not try to show everything at once.

Important lesson:

Minimalism here is not only visual. It is editorial.

## Blog Post Observations

The attached article screenshot adds an important second layer to the reference. The homepage is not the full story. His article layout shows how the same minimalist philosophy scales into long-form content.

### 1. The article keeps the same narrow-column discipline

The post body stays inside a very narrow reading width, similar to the homepage.

Effect:

- reading feels focused and intimate
- diagrams and embeds feel intentionally inserted, not just dropped into a CMS stream
- the page remains calm even when it becomes long

### 2. The typography remains restrained even in editorial mode

The article does not suddenly become loud or magazine-like.

Observed behavior:

- headings are clear but still modest
- body copy remains compact
- paragraphs are short and digestible
- captions and supporting UI stay subdued

Effect:

- the writing feels thoughtful rather than performative
- the page looks like notes from an expert, not content marketing

### 3. Visuals are embedded as proof, not decoration

The post contains:

- small demos
- charts/graphs
- code snippets
- screenshots
- simple interactive-looking panels

What stands out is that these visuals are used as evidence and explanation, not just as visual variety.

Effect:

- every visual block feels justified
- the article builds trust through demonstration
- the design stays minimal because media is functional

The article link confirms that many of these media blocks are not just images. They are interactive explanatory examples:

- origin-aware animation demos
- easing comparisons
- spring interaction demos
- tabs/clip-path examples
- small playground/code sections

This matters because the page is not "minimal" by removing richness. It is minimal by making every rich element purposeful.

### 4. Spacing controls complexity

Even though the article contains many different content types, it still feels clean because spacing is doing most of the organizational work.

Observed pattern:

- generous gaps between topic blocks
- enough margin before and after embeds
- clear separation between prose and examples

Effect:

- complexity never feels cluttered
- the article remains easy to scan
- each example feels self-contained

### 5. Embedded surfaces are extremely clean

The demos and media surfaces appear:

- lightly framed
- softly bordered
- not overly shadowed
- neutral in palette

Effect:

- examples feel integrated into the page
- the article remains visually quiet
- the UI inside the article is allowed to stand out without the page competing with it

### 6. Code is treated as part of the design language

The code blocks and implementation visuals do not feel like a separate theme pasted into the article.

Effect:

- technical content feels native to the site
- reinforces the "design engineer" identity
- makes code feel elegant rather than utilitarian

The live article also confirms that code and demos are interleaved in a very calm way:

- text explains
- example proves
- code supports

That sequence is important.

### 7. Long-form content still feels curated

Despite the article length, it does not feel bloated.

Likely reasons:

- short, intentional subsections
- examples placed at the right moments
- no unnecessary decorative components between sections
- strong visual consistency across all embedded blocks

The live article structure also shows that he writes in compact, focused subsections instead of large chapter-like sections. That keeps momentum high.

Observed pattern from the article:

- short heading
- short explanation
- embedded example
- short follow-up explanation

This pattern repeats cleanly.

### 8. The page proves that minimal does not mean empty

This is a very important takeaway for our project.

The homepage reference shows minimal identity.
The article reference shows minimal density.

Together they show that:

- a page can contain a lot of material
- and still feel clean
- if width, spacing, typography, and surface treatment are disciplined

## Blog-Specific Takeaways For Our Portfolio

If Mohamed's portfolio includes writing or case-study content, this reference suggests:

1. Keep article width narrow.
2. Use visuals only when they explain something.
3. Make demos/screenshots feel lightly framed and integrated.
4. Keep typography understated even in long-form content.
5. Let spacing organize the reading experience.
6. Treat code snippets as part of the aesthetic, not just utility blocks.
7. Avoid loud article templates with oversized banners, metadata blocks, and decorative dividers.
8. Use interactive examples only when they clarify a point.
9. Keep article sections short enough that the reader always feels progress.
10. Let explanation-example-code form a repeatable editorial rhythm.

## Combined Homepage + Blog Insight

- homepage: curated list
- blog: curated explanation

The system is the same:

- narrow width
- quiet type
- strong spacing
- functional visuals
- almost no decorative excess
- high trust in the reader's attention span
- low reliance on marketing-style framing

That consistency is one of the biggest reasons the overall body of work feels refined.

## What Makes It Feel Non-Cliché

This is the key reference value.

It avoids common portfolio clichés:

- no giant self-descriptive headline
- no overloaded hero section
- no wall of tech-stack badges
- no boxed section cards everywhere
- no fake case-study language
- no overly polished "personal brand" voice
- no aggressive CTA structure
- no visual tricks trying to create importance

Instead, it feels like:

- a highly curated personal index
- a calm professional notebook
- a designer-engineer's public workspace

## What To Borrow For Our Portfolio

These are the strongest ideas worth adapting:

1. Use a narrow central column.
2. Let spacing define section boundaries instead of cards.
3. Keep copy short and information-dense.
4. Make projects and writing read like curated lists, not marketing blocks.
5. Keep typography modest and confident.
6. Use very little decorative UI.
7. Make the page feel authored, not templated.
8. Prefer editorial calm over startup-brand energy.

## What Not To Copy Literally

We should borrow the philosophy, not clone the site.

Avoid copying directly:

- exact spacing values
- exact structure order
- exact typography proportions
- exact wording pattern
- identical list layout without adapting it to Mohamed's content

## Translation For Our Project

For Mohamed's portfolio, this reference suggests:

- a compact identity block instead of a giant hero
- very few section types
- project and writing lists that are text-led
- restrained use of color
- clean white or nearly white background
- soft muted secondary copy
- strong emphasis on rhythm, alignment, and curation

## One-Line Summary
