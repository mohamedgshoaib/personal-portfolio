## Overall Impression

This design is also minimal, but in a different way from Emil Kowalski's site.

Emil feels like a curated editorial column.
Shu Ding feels like a sparse personal archive.

The site is:

- more structural
- more referential
- more index-like
- more academic / notebook-like
- more detached and timeless

It is not trying to "present a brand". It feels like a durable place for writing, work, and references.

## Core Visual Characteristics

### 1. Persistent left-side navigation rail

The most defining visual element is the small left-side navigation column:

- `about`
- `thoughts`
- `projects`

It sits apart from the main content and creates a consistent site frame.

Effect:

- gives the whole site a strong sense of structure
- makes the experience feel like a personal document system
- replaces the need for a hero or page chrome
- creates identity through layout instead of decoration

### 2. A vertical rule separates navigation from content

The screenshots show a thin vertical divider between the navigation rail and the main content column.

Effect:

- creates a very subtle editorial grid
- makes the page feel designed without obvious ornament
- adds discipline and order while remaining quiet

### 3. Narrow content column, but left-aligned rather than centered

Unlike Emil's centered narrow column, Shu's content is offset with a fixed content area to the right of the nav.

Effect:

- feels more like a studio notebook or reading system
- gives the page a stable, architectural layout
- makes the site feel more archival and less landing-page-like

An important part of this feeling is how much empty space remains on the right. The content does not try to "fill the canvas". The unused space becomes part of the design.

### 4. Very restrained color palette

The site appears almost monochrome:

- white background
- dark gray text
- muted gray secondary text
- underlined or lightly emphasized links

Effect:

- all emphasis comes from typography and position
- nothing feels decorative
- supports longevity and seriousness

### 5. No card-based UI

Across the homepage, thoughts page, article page, and projects page, the site avoids modern portfolio conventions such as:

- project cards
- badges
- pills
- icons used as decoration
- boxed sections
- colored surfaces

Effect:

- the site feels timeless
- the content has to stand on its own
- everything reads like published notes rather than portfolio marketing

## Homepage Observations

The homepage is very simple:

- name
- concise introduction
- a few contextual links

It does not attempt to summarize everything at once.

### Tone of the homepage

The tone is:

- factual
- calm
- lightly intellectual
- personal without oversharing
- quietly credible

It avoids:

- dramatic positioning statements
- exaggerated self-description
- emotional copywriting
- startup-style credibility stacking

### Structural takeaway

The homepage reads almost like a well-written "about" note rather than a homepage.

That is a big reason it feels clean.

## Thoughts Index Observations

The thoughts page is especially useful as a reference.

### 1. It is just a list

The writing archive is presented as a clean list of titles and dates.

Observed pattern:

- title on the left
- date on the right
- dotted or faint connecting line between them
- no excerpt cards
- no thumbnail previews
- no category chips

Effect:

- feels bibliographic
- makes writing feel substantial
- gives a lot of information with very little UI

The live index also confirms that the archive is title-only. There are no excerpts or preview summaries competing for attention.

### 2. Dates are visually important

Dates are consistently visible and aligned, which gives the archive a historical quality.

Effect:

- emphasizes the body of work over promotion
- makes the writing feel cumulative
- helps the list feel like an index, not a feed

The archive also mixes English and Chinese post titles naturally, which makes the page feel like a real long-lived personal notebook rather than a curated content funnel.

### 3. The current section state is subtle

The active navigation item is shown through small contrast changes, not loud highlights.

Effect:

- preserves the quiet design language
- keeps navigation functional without turning it into UI chrome

The screenshots also suggest the navigation labels are slightly italic and lightly toned by default. That small typographic move gives the rail personality without making it feel designed-for-attention.

## Article Page Observations

The `Good Design` article is important because it shows how this system handles long-form thought.

### 1. Article layout is still very plain

The page keeps the same left rail and the same restrained content area.

Effect:

- article pages feel like part of the same system, not a separate blog engine
- continuity across the site is very strong

### 2. Typography is simple and sober

The article uses:

- modest title sizing
- comfortable but not oversized line-height
- compact paragraph rhythm
- traditional long-form reading behavior

Effect:

- the writing feels serious and durable
- the page trusts the text rather than styling it heavily

### 3. Images are large, dark, and integral to the argument

The `Good Design` article uses large black charts/graphics embedded directly in the content flow.

Important observation:

- these images are not decorative accents
- they function as conceptual diagrams
- they punctuate the prose without breaking the overall calm

Effect:

- gives the article intellectual weight
- creates strong visual pauses
- makes the page memorable while still minimal

The diagrams are especially effective because they are not polished illustrations. They look closer to conceptual whiteboard plots translated into a precise visual form.

### 4. The page can handle density because the frame stays unchanged

Even as the article gets longer and includes multiple large visuals, it still feels clean because:

- the navigation rail stays fixed as a structural anchor
- the content width is disciplined
- spacing between paragraphs and diagrams is generous
- there is no additional UI clutter

### 5. Minimalism here is more severe than Emil's

Compared to Emil:

- fewer flourishes
- less playful rhythm
- less "crafted interaction" energy
- more emphasis on writing and thought structure

This is useful because it shows a more austere end of the same design spectrum.

## Projects Page Observations

The projects page is a very strong portfolio reference.

### 1. Projects are presented as a text archive, not a showcase gallery

The project list is almost entirely text-based.

Observed pattern:

- project title
- short paragraph
- occasional inline links
- grouped lists for major work vs smaller work

Effect:

- the portfolio feels serious and cumulative
- the user is invited to read, not just browse cards
- depth of work becomes the main signal

The live page also shows that several major entries are introduced with logos or marks rather than oversized project-name headings. This gives each section some identity while keeping the overall page extremely restrained.

### 2. Major projects and smaller projects are separated editorially

Instead of putting everything into one repeated card system, the page distinguishes:

- major project writeups
- fun experiments
- mini projects

Effect:

- creates a more honest hierarchy
- avoids giving equal visual weight to everything
- helps the body of work feel curated

The separators are editorial rather than componentized. Thin horizontal rules and list breaks do the organizing work instead of reusable card shells.

### 3. Inline links do a lot of work

The page uses inline links within prose rather than separate CTA rows.

Effect:

- keeps the layout calm
- reduces interface noise
- makes the portfolio feel like authored writing

## Typography Notes

### Tone

Typography feels:

- literary
- archival
- quiet
- highly controlled

It does not feel:

- product-marketing-oriented
- trendy
- highly branded
- performatively minimal

### Hierarchy

Hierarchy comes from:

- placement
- indentation / column behavior
- subtle weight changes
- date alignment
- whitespace
- occasional wordmarks / marks for major projects

Not from:

- giant headings
- bold section banners
- large hero text
- contrasting background blocks

## What Makes It Feel Non-Cliché

This reference avoids portfolio clichés through structure and restraint:

- no hero section
- no project cards
- no over-designed navigation
- no loud brand system
- no testimonial/social-proof sections
- no artificial "selected works" framing
- no decorative gradients or abstract backgrounds

It feels more like:

- a personal intellectual archive
- a quiet reading room
- a long-lived personal website

## What To Borrow For Our Portfolio

These are the strongest ideas to adapt:

1. Use structure as the identity.
2. Let navigation be small and quiet.
3. Present writing as an index, not a content grid.
4. Present projects as thoughtful text, not cards.
5. Use dates and alignment to create rhythm.
6. Keep everything monochrome or near-monochrome by default.
7. Let the page feel like a personal archive rather than a landing page.
8. Use editorial grouping instead of repeated UI patterns.

## What Not To Copy Literally

We should borrow the philosophy, not duplicate the exact site.

Avoid copying directly:

- the exact left-rail proportions
- the exact dotted list treatment
- the exact typography scale
- the exact archival tone without adapting it to Mohamed's personality
- the exact sparse content density if Mohamed needs slightly warmer presentation

## Contrast With Emil Kowalski

This comparison is useful for later synthesis.

Emil:

- curated editorial portfolio
- more crafted and warm
- slightly more design-engineer energy
- more list rhythm and explanatory polish

Shu:

- personal archive / notebook system
- more austere and structural
- more academic / timeless
- stronger use of layout frame as identity

Both avoid cliché portfolio patterns, but they get there differently.

## Translation For Our Project

For Mohamed's portfolio, this reference suggests:

- consider a persistent structural layout element instead of a conventional navbar
- writing should feel like a clean archive
- projects can be text-led and hierarchy-driven instead of card-driven
- strong layout discipline can replace visual decoration
- understated design can still feel highly authored

## One-Line Summary

Shu Ding's site feels exceptional because it turns a portfolio into a quiet, durable archive where layout structure replaces almost all decorative design.
